Promoting Well-being For All At All Ages

SDG 3: Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages

About SDG 3

Real progress starts with good health.

We are fully committed to stopping the spread of serious infections, fighting chronic illnesses, making sure that everyone—no matter who they are—can see a doctor when they need to.

We believe quality healthcare is a fundamental human right, not a luxury for the lucky few.

After all, you cannot have a healthy economy without healthy people.

Our Goals

By 2030, we are targeting specific milestones to transform global health:

Maternal & Child Health: Reduce the global maternal mortality ratio to less than 70 per 100,000 live births and end preventable deaths of newborns and children under 5.

Fight Communicable Diseases: End the epidemics of AIDS, tuberculosis, malaria, and neglected tropical diseases, while combatting hepatitis and water-borne diseases.

Tackle Non-Communicable Diseases: Reduce premature mortality from non-communicable diseases (like heart disease and diabetes) by one-third through prevention and treatment, and promote mental health and well-being.

Road Safety: Halve the number of global deaths and injuries from road traffic accidents.

The Challenges We Face

While we have made strides, the world is currently off track to meet many of these targets by 2030

Progress is Too Slow: Although maternal mortality has declined, it is not dropping fast enough. Nearly 800 women still die every day from preventable causes related to pregnancy and childbirth.

Infectious Disease Threats: Tuberculosis cases are rising—2023 saw the highest number of new cases since monitoring began. While HIV deaths have dropped, new infections are rising in several regions.

Vaccination Gaps: Childhood vaccination coverage has stagnated or dipped. In 2021 alone, 25 million children missed out on routine immunizations.

Workforce Shortages: We face a projected global shortage of 10 million health workers by 2030, with the most critical gaps in low-income countries.

Prevention is Better Than Cure

Invest in Health Systems: Governments must invest substantially in recruiting, training, and retaining health workers.

Commit to UHC: We must accelerate efforts toward Universal Health Coverage to ensure that financial status does not dictate health outcomes.

Restore Immunization: We need immediate action to bring vaccination rates back to pre-pandemic levels and ensure no child is missed.

Research & Development: We must continue to support the research and development of vaccines and medicines for diseases that primarily affect developing countries
